# Understanding Tides with simulation

## **Lesson Plan: Understanding Tides**

---

### **Subject: Earth Science
Grade Level: 6-8
Time Allotment: 60-75 minutes**

[https://effectuall.github.io/Simulations/TidalWaves/](https://effectuall.github.io/Simulations/TidalWaves/)

## **I. Learning Objectives:**

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Define what tides are and identify their primary cause.
2. Explain the gravitational forces of the Moon and Sun on Earth's oceans.
3. Differentiate between high tide and low tide.
4. Describe the difference between spring tides and neap tides.
5. Identify the factors that influence local tide patterns.
****

## **II. Materials:**

- Whiteboard or projector
- Markers or pens
- Computer with internet access (for videos/simulations)
- World map or globe
- Optional: Beach ball or large inflatable globe, small ball (representing the Moon), flashlight (representing the Sun)
- Student handouts: "Tide Tracker" worksheet (optional, for observation activity)
- Optional: Access to real-time tide data websites (e.g., NOAA Tides & Currents)

### **III. Procedure:**

**A. Engage (10 minutes)**

1. **Hook:** Begin by asking students if they've ever been to the beach and noticed the water level change throughout the day.
    ◦ "What did you observe about the water level at different times?"
    ◦ "Why do you think this happens?"

2. **Brainstorm:** Have students share their initial ideas about what causes the ocean water to rise and fall. Record their ideas on the board.

3. **Introduce the term "Tides":** Explain that these regular rises and falls of ocean water are called tides.
****

### **B. Explore (20 minutes)**

1. **Demonstration:**
    ◦ Use a large ball (Earth) and a smaller ball (Moon). Hold the Moon ball near the Earth ball.
    ◦ Ask students to imagine the Moon's gravity pulling on the Earth.
    ◦ *Teacher Note:* Emphasize that gravity is a [force of attraction between any two objects](https://effectuall.github.io/Simulations/Mechanics_Gravitational_Force.html) with mass. The closer the objects, the stronger the pull.
    ◦ Explain how the Moon's gravity pulls on the side of Earth closest to it, causing a bulge of water.
    ◦ Then, explain the bulge on the opposite side (inertia/Earth being pulled away from the water on that side).
2. **Simulation:** 
    
     ◦ Simulate the tide actions  
    
    - **2D Tide Simulation:** [[2D Simulation Link](https://effectuall.github.io/Simulations/TidalWaves/)]
    - **3D Tide Simulation:** [Contact Us](https://www.notion.so/Contact-Us-1e80bb5858c48056a547dd410aaaf0d9?pvs=21)
3. **Visual Aid/Video:** Show a short animated video or simulation explaining the Moon's gravitational pull on Earth and the creation of two tidal bulges.
    ◦ *Suggested search terms:* "tides explained animation," "gravitational pull of moon on earth tides."
****

### **C. Explain (20 minutes)**

1. **Define High and Low Tides:**
    ◦ **High Tide:** Explain that when a location is within one of these bulges, it experiences a high tide (higher water level).
    ◦ **Low Tide:** As Earth rotates, locations between the bulges experience low tide (lower water level).
    ◦ Explain that most places experience two high tides and two low tides approximately every 24 hours and 50 minutes. (The extra 50 minutes is due to the Moon's orbit around Earth).
2. **The Sun's Influence:**
    ◦ Introduce the Sun's role. While the Sun is much larger, it's also much farther away, so its gravitational pull on Earth's tides is about half as strong as the Moon's.
    ◦ **Spring Tides:** Explain that when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (during new moon and full moon phases), their gravitational pulls combine, resulting in *extraordinarily high high tides* and *extraordinarily low low tides*. These are called **spring tides** (no relation to the season).
    ◦ **Neap Tides:** Explain that when the Sun, Moon, and Earth form a right angle (during first and third quarter moon phases), their gravitational pulls work against each other, resulting in *less extreme high tides* and *less extreme low tides*. These are called **neap tides**.
3. **Factors Influencing Local Tides:** Briefly mention that local geography (coastline shape, ocean depth) can also affect how tides behave in specific areas.
****

### **D. Elaborate (15 minutes)**

1. **Tide Chart Analysis (Optional):** If time and resources allow, show students a real-time tide chart for a local coastal area or a well-known location.
    ◦ Ask them to identify the times of high and low tide.
    ◦ Discuss how the patterns change over several days.
2. **"Tide Tracker" Worksheet:** Distribute a simple worksheet where students can draw or describe the positions of the Earth, Moon, and Sun for both spring and neap tides, labeling high and low tide locations.
****

### **E. Evaluate (5 minutes)**

1. **Quick Check for Understanding:**
    ◦ "What is the main cause of tides?" (Moon's gravity)
    ◦ "When do we experience spring tides?" (New and Full Moon, when Sun, Earth, Moon are aligned)
    ◦ "When do we experience neap tides?" (First and Third Quarter Moon, when Sun, Earth, Moon form a right angle)
    ◦ "How many high tides and low tides do most places experience in a day?" (Two of each)
2. **Exit Ticket:** Have students write down one new thing they learned about tides and one question they still have.
****

### **IV. Differentiation:**

• **Support:**
    ◦ Provide graphic organizers for note-taking.
    ◦ Pair students for the "Explore" activity.
    ◦ Offer simplified diagrams of tidal forces.
    ◦ Use sentence starters for written responses.
• **Challenge:**
    ◦ Research the tidal bore phenomenon or other unique tidal occurrences.
    ◦ Investigate how tides are used for generating electricity (tidal power).
    ◦ Explore how tides affect marine life.
****

### **V. Assessment:**

• **Formative:**
    ◦ Observations during the "Engage" and "Explore" activities.
    ◦ Responses to quick check questions.
    ◦ Completion of the "Tide Tracker" worksheet.
    ◦ Exit Ticket responses.

• **Summative (Optional):**
    ◦ Short quiz on tide definitions and types.
    ◦ Diagramming the Earth-Moon-Sun system for different tide types.
****

### **VI. Homework/Extension Activities:**

• **Tide Observation:** If possible, have students observe local tide times (from a newspaper, website, or app) for a week and record their findings.
• **Research:** Research the highest tides in the world (e.g., Bay of Fundy) or the impact of tides on coastal ecosystems.
• **Creative Project:** Create a model or a short presentation explaining tides to a younger audience.
